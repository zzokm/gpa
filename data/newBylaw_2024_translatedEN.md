<a name="2-vision-mission-objectives"></a>
## 2. Vision, Mission, Core Values & Objectives

### Faculty Vision
> "To be the best local and regional source for academic education, scientific and applied research in the fields of computing, informatics, and decision support."

### Faculty Mission
> "The Faculty of Computers and Artificial Intelligence - Cairo University is committed to providing quality education and research to create highly competitive frameworks of specialists in the fields of informatics, computing, and decision support. The faculty also provides programs for capacity building and offers consultations and technical solutions contributing to social and economic development."

### Governing Core Values
The governing values of the faculty's performance are represented by the acronym **MATCH**, which stands for the following five values:
1. **Market Oriented**
2. **Accountability**
3. **Transparency**
4. **Competitiveness**
5. **Academic Honesty**

### Strategic Objectives
1. Raising the skills of the faculty graduate and increasing the competitiveness of students and researchers.
2. Continuous development of the capabilities of faculty members and their assistants.
3. Providing the standard environment and exemplary climate for educational and research processes.
4. Developing, supporting, and investing in scientific research to enhance local and regional public and government services.
5. Strengthening partnership relations and its programs with civil society institutions and environmental development.
6. Commitment to applying quality standards and periodically evaluating institutional capacity and educational effectiveness.

### Objectives of the Amendment
The amendment of the faculty bylaw aims to achieve the following:
1. Building an advanced educational system that accommodates the rapid growth in the fields of computers and information.
2. Keeping pace with modern scientific trends in the faculty’s specializations by updating the study courses.
3. Providing greater space for advanced practical training to match the labor requirements in all state institutions.
4. Allowing students freedom of choice for the specializations they wish to study in accordance with their inclinations and interests.
5. Providing the appropriate scientific climate for faculty members and researchers to pioneer new and advanced scientific specializations.
6. Providing an opportunity to distinguish between the different capabilities of students and enabling outstanding students to achieve their scientific ambitions.

## 5. Academic Requirements and Regulations

<a name="art-1"></a>
### Article (1) Study System
* **A-** Study in the offered programs relies on the credit hours system. The academic year is divided into two regular semesters, and the credit hour is the educational unit of measurement to determine the weight of the course.
* **B-** The Faculty Council may approve holding intensive summer semesters for some courses based on the proposal of the academic departments and according to the capabilities and circumstances of the faculty.
* **C-** The faculty grants a Bachelor's degree in Computers and Artificial Intelligence in one of the specializations listed in the bylaw once the student fulfills the requirements for obtaining it as stipulated by the faculty regulations:
    * The student must successfully pass one hundred and thirty-five credit hours (135 credit hours) with a Cumulative Grade Point Average ($CGPA$) of not less than 2.0.
    * The student must successfully pass all non-credit courses stipulated in the faculty bylaw.
    * The minimum limit for graduation (obtaining the Bachelor's degree) is three academic years, meaning six regular semesters (Fall and Spring), once the student meets the requirements for obtaining it.
    * Passing what the university stipulates as graduation requirements.
* **D-** The Faculty Council, after taking the opinion of the relevant department council and according to the nature of the courses, may decide to teach one or more courses using the hybrid learning model, such that the study in the course is 70% face-to-face and 30% through distance learning, or by any other ratio, provided that this is presented to the University Education and Student Affairs Council for approval and raised to the University Council for endorsement.
* **E-** The student has the right to choose the specialization at the beginning of the semester after passing 45 hours, according to the rules specified by the faculty administration.

<a name="art-2"></a>
### Article (2) Language of Instruction
Study in the offered programs is conducted in both Arabic and English according to the requirements and specifications of each course.

<a name="art-3"></a>
### Article (3) Academic Advising
The faculty assigns an academic advisor from the faculty members to each group of students. The advisor performs academic advising tasks, helps the student choose courses to study, registers them, and guides the student throughout their study period at the faculty. The academic advisor’s opinion is advisory, and the student is solely responsible for the courses registered based on their preference.

<a name="art-4"></a>
### Article (4) Registration, Drop and Add
* **A-** At the beginning of each semester, the student registers the selected courses through the faculty website during the times specified by the faculty administration before regular classes begin, according to the following schedule:
    * **First Week:** Regular registration of courses for students.
    * **Second Week:** Late registration with payment of a fine.
    * **Third and Fourth Weeks:** Withdrawal of courses.
* **B-** The Faculty Council determines the minimum and maximum number of students allowed to register in each course.
* **C- Number of Registration Hours:**
    * **For regular semesters:**
        * The minimum limit of credit hours for registration is (9) hours. The minimum limit may be bypassed if the remaining hours for the student to graduate are less than 9.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours for students with a starting semester $GPA$ greater than or equal to 2.0 is 18 credit hours.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours for students with a starting semester $GPA$ greater than 1.0 and less than 2.0 is 15 credit hours.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours for students with a starting semester $GPA$ less than or equal to 1.0 is 12 credit hours.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours can be increased up to 21 credit hours in the following cases:
            1. Fourth-level students with a starting semester $GPA$ greater than or equal to 2.0.
            2. Students of any level with a starting semester $GPA$ greater than or equal to 3.0.
            3. Students wishing to register a course in which they previously received an incomplete grade, provided their starting semester $GPA$ is greater than or equal to 2.0.
    * **For the summer semester:**
        * It is a compressed semester lasting 7 weeks, where the weekly contact hours for the course are doubled.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours for students is 6 credit hours.
        * The maximum limit of registered hours can be increased to 9 credit hours for graduation purposes.
* **D-** After completing registration procedures, the student may drop or add one or more courses during the period specified by the faculty for dropping and adding. This is done in coordination with the student's academic advisor via the faculty website.
* **E-** The student is allowed to study different courses and register for higher-level courses based on selecting the required prerequisites for those higher courses. A student cannot be registered in a higher course unless they pass its prerequisites.

<a name="art-5"></a>
### Article (5) Course Withdrawal
* **A-** After registering the selected courses, a student may withdraw from one or more courses during the period specified in Article 4, provided that the student's registered hours do not fall below the minimum registration limit for a single semester. In this case, the student is not considered to have failed the withdrawn courses, and a grade of "Withdrawn" ("$W$") is recorded.
* **B-** If a student withdraws from one or more courses after the specified period without a compelling excuse accepted by the Faculty Council, a grade of "Fail" ("$F$") is recorded for the withdrawn courses.

<a name="art-6"></a>
### Article (6) Attendance and Absence
* **A-** Study in the program is regular, and enrollment by external affiliation (Entisab) is not permitted. Attendance monitoring is subject to conditions and regulations set by the faculty administration.
* **B-** Admission to the final exam requires achieving an attendance rate of not less than 75% of lectures and tutorials in each course. If a student's absence rate - without an accepted excuse - exceeds 25% in a course, the Faculty Council has the right to bar them from entering the final exam. A grade of "zero" is given for the final exam score of the course. However, if the student submits an excuse accepted by the Faculty Council within the period specified by the Council, a grade of "Withdrawn" is recorded for that course.
* **C-** A student who absents themselves from the final exam of any course without an accepted excuse is given a grade of "zero" in that exam, and must retake the course completely.
* **D- Incomplete Grade regulations ($I$):**
    * If a student submits a compelling excuse accepted by the Faculty Council for not attending the final exam of any course before or within two days of the final exam, a grade of "Incomplete" ("$I$") is recorded for this course, provided that the student has obtained at least 60% of the course work marks, and has not been barred from final exams. The student with an "Incomplete" grade is allowed to take the final exam only. The student's final mark is calculated based on the score obtained in the final exam plus the score previously obtained in the course work. The student must take the final exam during the same academic year or the following academic year from recording the course as incomplete; otherwise, the student must retake the course entirely without counting the previously obtained course work marks.
* **E-** If a student submits a compelling excuse accepted by the Faculty Council for not attending the final exam of any course within two days of the exam, and the condition of obtaining at least 60% of the course work marks is not met, a grade of "Withdrawn" is recorded for the course.

<a name="art-7"></a>
### Article (7) Interruption of Study
* **A-** A student is considered to have interrupted their studies if they do not register for a semester or withdraw from all courses of the semester without an accepted excuse. In case the excuse is rejected, the semester is counted within the allowable semesters for the student to pass the registered level.
* **B-** A student may interrupt their studies - with an accepted excuse - for a maximum of two consecutive semesters or three non-consecutive semesters.
* **C-** A student may submit a request to freeze registration at the faculty according to the terms and conditions established by the university.

<a name="art-8"></a>
### Article (8) Dismissal from the Faculty
A student is dismissed from the faculty in accordance with the failure opportunities stipulated in the Executive Regulations of the Universities Organization Law, which specifies the number of years granted to the student as follows:
* **First-level students:** Two years.
* **Second-level students:** Two years + one exceptional year with the approval of the Faculty Council.
* **Third-level students:** Two years + three exceptional years with the approval of the Faculty Council.
* **Fourth-level students:** Two years + three exceptional years with the approval of the Faculty Council.
* **Note:** Registration freezes approved by the Faculty Council are not counted within the allowed years.

<a name="art-9"></a>
### Article (9) Examination System
* **A-** The maximum mark for any course is (100) marks.
* **B-** The minimum mark for passing a course is 50% of the total marks of the course, and at least 30% of the final exam marks.
* **C- Examination marks for each course are distributed as follows:**
    * **Course work:** 40% allocated to course work distributed between:
        * Midterm exam with a maximum of 20%.
        * Other exams conducted periodically by the instructor, practical applications, and assignments tasked during the semester, and oral exams may be added.
    * **Final Examination:** 60% allocated to the end-of-semester exam.
    * The Faculty Council determines and announces the dates for midterm and final exams in a timely manner.
* **D-** If the final exam of a course includes a written test and a practical test based on the proposal of department councils and approval of the Faculty Council, the student's final exam mark consists of the total sum of both the written and practical tests.
* **E-** The Faculty Council, after taking the opinion of the relevant department council and according to the nature of the courses, may decide to conduct the exam electronically for one or more courses. The exam can be held for the whole course or part of it to allow electronic grading, provided it is presented to the University Education and Student Affairs Council for approval and raised to the University Council for endorsement.

<a name="art-10"></a>
### Article (10) Grading and Evaluation System
* **A-** The faculty follows the credit hours system, where the basic unit is the course and not the academic year. The grading system is based on the grade of each course using points determined according to the following table:

| Percentage Mark | Grade | Points ($Points$) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| 96% and above | +A | 4.0 |
| 92% - less than 96% | A | 3.7 |
| 88% - less than 92% | -A | 3.4 |
| 84% - less than 88% | +B | 3.2 |
| 80% - less than 84% | B | 3.0 |
| 76% - less than 80% | -B | 2.8 |
| 72% - less than 76% | +C | 2.6 |
| 68% - less than 72% | C | 2.4 |
| 64% - less than 68% | -C | 2.2 |
| 60% - less than 64% | +D | 2.0 |
| 55% - less than 60% | D | 1.5 |
| 50% - less than 55% | -D | 1.0 |
| less than 50% | F | zero |
| Absent from final exam without accepted excuse | Abs | zero |
| Continuous course in next semester | Con | Without points, hours are not calculated in cumulative average until course is completed via passing or failing |
| Incomplete course | I | Follows same rule above |
| Withdrawn course | W | Follows same rule above |

**Passing and Failing non-credit courses:**
Grade table for non-credit courses:

| Grade | Meaning |
| :---: | :--- |
| AU | Auditor |
| P | Pass |
| F | Fail |
| W | Withdrawn |
| Abs | Absent from final exam without accepted excuse from Faculty Council |
| I | Incomplete (if course has course work marks) |

* **B- The Cumulative Grade Point Average ($CGPA$) is calculated as follows:**
    * Total points are calculated by: multiplying the value of the grade of each course (points shown in table) by the number of credit hours of that course to get the points for each course.
    * Summing up the points of all courses registered by the student.
    * Dividing total points by total registered hours to obtain the cumulative grade point average as follows:
$$\text{CGPA} = \frac{\text{Total Points}}{\text{Total Registered Hours}}$$
* **C-** The general grade for each semester and upon graduation is calculated based on the cumulative grade point average obtained by the student according to the following table:

| Cumulative Average | General Grade |
| :--- | :--- |
| less than 1.0 | Very Weak |
| 1.0 - less than 2.0 | Weak |
| 2.0 - less than 2.5 | Pass |
| 2.5 - less than 3.0 | Good |
| 3.0 - less than 3.5 | Very Good |
| 3.5 and above | Excellent |

* **D- Honors Degree:** The student is awarded an honors degree if they pass the academic units studied at each level with a grade of not less than "Very Good", provided that the total period of study does not exceed four academic years.

<a name="art-11"></a>
### Article (11) Failure, Repetition & Improvement

#### A- Retaking a course a student failed previously:
* If a student fails a course, they must retake the course and retake its exam. If they pass the course after retaking it, the actual score obtained is counted, up to a maximum of 83 marks, which is the highest score for a ($B$) grade.
* The cumulative average is calculated on this basis, counting the course credit hours only once.
* All repetition attempts and the score or grade obtained in each attempt appear clearly in the student's transcript.
* The student pays for retaking the course an amount equivalent to the fees paid for course registration in the summer semester.

#### B- Retaking a course a student previously passed with points less than 2.0 to raise the cumulative average to avoid dismissal:
* If a student obtains a cumulative grade point average ($CGPA$) at the beginning of the semester of less than 2.0, the student is under academic probation and must raise their average.
* The student mentioned above under academic probation has the right to repeat any course they previously passed with points less than (2) to raise their cumulative average to avoid dismissal. They must re-study and retake its exam. In this case, the student receives the highest mark obtained among all repetition attempts, up to a maximum of 83 marks (highest mark in grade $B$).
* The repeated course must belong to the level in which the student is currently enrolled, or belong to one level lower.
* There is no maximum limit for these courses; a student under academic probation can repeat any number of courses previously passed with points less than (2) to raise their cumulative grade point average ($CGPA$) to 2.0.
* The cumulative average is calculated on this basis, counting the course credit hours once.
* All repetition attempts and the grade appear in the transcript. The student pays a fee equivalent to summer semester fees.

#### C- Retaking a course a student previously passed with points greater than or equal to 2.0 to raise the cumulative average for improvement:
* A student with a cumulative average greater than or equal to 2.0 has the right to improve their average only if they have not completed the required number of graduation hours.
* If a student wishes to repeat a course they passed with points greater than or equal to (2) to raise their average, they must re-study it and retake its exam. In this case, they receive the highest grade obtained among attempts.
* The maximum number of hours allowed for improvement for courses where the student scored points greater than or equal to 2 is nine hours, whether for one course or multiple courses.
* The highest score obtained among attempts is counted up to a maximum of 83 marks (highest score in grade $B$).
* The maximum limit for repeating courses previously passed with points greater than or equal to (2) for average improvement is three courses.
* The course must belong to the level in which the student is currently enrolled, or belong to a level lower or higher by one level.
* The cumulative average is calculated on this basis, counting the course hours once.
* All repetition attempts and grades appear in the transcript. The student pays a fee equivalent to summer semester fees.

<a name="art-12"></a>
### Article (12) Regulatory Provisions
* **A-** Each department prepares a complete specification of the contents of the courses it teaches, and these contents are presented to the Faculty Council. After approval by the Faculty Council, these contents become binding for the faculty members teaching those courses.
* **B-** The Faculty Council, based on the proposal of relevant department councils, may modify registration requirements for any course, as well as its scientific content, provided modifications do not exceed 25% of the original core content.

<a name="art-13"></a>
### Article (13) Progression Between Academic Levels
The student's level is determined at the beginning of the academic year as follows:
* **A- First Level:** The student is registered at the first level upon joining the faculty and remains at the first level as long as they have not passed 27 credit hours.
* **B- Second Level:** The student transitions from the first level to the second level upon passing 27 credit hours.
* **C- Third Level:** The student transitions from the second level to the third level upon passing 60 credit hours.
* **D- Fourth Level:** The student transitions from the third level to the fourth level upon passing 96 credit hours.

<a name="art-14"></a>
### Article (14) Implementation of the Bylaw
* **A-** The provisions of this bylaw apply to fresh students at the beginning of the academic year following its approval.
* **B-** The provisions of the Universities Organization Law and its Executive Regulations apply to matters not covered by a text in this bylaw.

## 7. Appendix 1: Course Contents

### First: General Requirements (12 credit hours)
#### A. Compulsory Courses (6 compulsory credit hours distributed as follows):

* **HU111: Technical Report Writing**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* The basic rudiments of report writing; the rationale for report writing; the structure of reports and such details as physical appearance and linguistic style; writing reports.

* **HU112: Ethics and Professionalism**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Critical examination of ethical problems associated with computer science and engineering; legal and quasi-legal (i.e. policy and regulative) issues; Process of ethical decision-making; Privacy and confidentiality; Computer crime; Professional codes and responsibilities; Software piracy; Impact of computers on society.

* **HU124: Critical Thinking**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Determine the position of critical thinking among the forms of human thinking and its different levels and patterns; Know what critical thinking is and its different skills; Discover common mistakes in thinking; Set the criteria for critical thinking; Discriminate between correct and incorrect thinking patterns; Apply critical thinking skills in different life situations; Apply critical and logical thinking in solving problems and making various decisions; Identify the validity of the arguments, and their strengths and weaknesses; Detect logical fallacies in thinking; Apply critical reading skills on what he reads in various fields; Apply critical thinking skills on what he receives from news and opinions via various mass media; Determine the nature of propaganda and media methods; Apply critical thinking skills to confront the media.

#### B. Elective Courses (6 hours chosen by the student from the following elective courses):

* **HU113: Creative Thinking and Communication Skills**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Meta-cognition (thinking about thinking); Edward de Bono's CoRT (cognitive research trust) program of learning thinking; Vertical and lateral thinking approaches; Creative thinking tools like Brainstorming, Tony Buzan's Mind mapping and Edward de Bono's Six Thinking hats. Theories of communication; How to translate theories into complete strategies to communicate with diverse audience; Written Communications: Memoranda, Letters, Executive summaries, Business and research reports; Oral Communications: Listening, Presentation skills, Interviewing, Conducting meetings, Interpersonal communication, Negotiation; Intercultural communication; Importance of communication in team building.

* **HU121: Fundamentals of Economics**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Concept of economics; the economic problem; Theory of demand including: utility theory, theory of production, theory of cost, theory of firm including: pricing theory; Economics of education; Economics of science and technology; Economics of automation including: computerization.

* **DS251: Fundamentals of Management**
  * *Prerequisite:* Passing 30 Credit Hours
  * *Description:* History of Management; planning, fundamentals of planning; making decisions; strategic planning; plans and planning tools; Organizing and managing human resources; Influencing; leadership; controlling; Production management and control; Quality management; Management of service industries; accounting for risk; and economic analysis.

* **HU123: Marketing and Sales**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Define marketing; Marketing process; Market analysis: customer base, competition; Best practices and lessons learned; Business research and forecasting tools and techniques; Trend analysis: economics, social, political, environmental, technology; Technology assessment practices and techniques; Presentation skills; Sales and advertising practices; Customer satisfaction strategies; Marketing and branding techniques; Product portfolio analysis; Global trade and international operations; Pricing strategies; Managing marketing through: customer relationships, social responsibility, marketing ethics; Implementation through Business Models and Technology Essentials; E-Commerce Application.

* **HU114: Fundamentals of Psychology**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Research methods; neurobiological foundations of behavior; learning; memory; personality; stress and its effect on well-being; abnormal behavior and pathology; and social psychology; social and emotional development; Abnormal behavior. General principles of psychology as they are applied to work relationships and self. Includes perception, learning, development, motivation, emotion, therapy, communication, attitudes.

* **HU115: Fundamentals of Sociology**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Basic concepts; Basic examination of major theoretical perspectives; Structural functionalism; Symbolic interactionism; conflict theory; Types of Society: Tribal, agrarian, industrial, Post-industrial; Culture; Social networks; Social institutions; Deviance; Education; Religion; Race and ethnicity; Social class; Socialization; Gender identity; Social construction of the family; Community; Health; Social processes; Social change; Social Problems; Social demography.

* **HU116: Comparative Politics**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Central concepts and methods in comparative studies; Political development and democratization; revolution; political culture; Comparison of different countries with respect to the founding principles of: Political system, Electoral system, Parities, Interest organizations, Parliament, Government, Public administration, Policy processes, Political economy; Internationalization.

* **HU117: Social Issues**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Introduction; Definition of human rights; historical development of the concept of human rights; culture relativism versus universally accepted human rights standards; various human rights: personal, political, civil, social, economical...etc.; covering human rights within official international organizations; influence of business and global economic restructuring on human rights; monitoring human rights; human rights violations.

* **HU118: Selected Topics in Humanities**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* This course aims at introducing students to interesting topics in humanities that need to be identified in a responsive manner to current time.

* **HU225: Entrepreneurship**
  * *Prerequisite:* Critical Thinking
  * *Description:* Understand the meaning of Entrepreneurship, its importance to the Entrepreneur and to the economy; Know the importance of an entrepreneurial idea, the means and sources of generate this idea; Identify the characteristics, aptitudes, and qualifications of effective Entrepreneur; Identify different areas and fields that Entrepreneur can choose among them to start his project; Prepare a feasibility study for the project; Formulate a strategic/business plan for the project; Manage the project professionally; Know the organizations that support and incubate the entrepreneurial projects.

### Second: Faculty Requirements (57 credit hours)
#### 1-2 Mathematics and Basic Sciences (21 compulsory credit hours distributed as follows):

* **MA111: Math-1**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Functions; Limits and Continuity; Definition of the derivative; Higher order derivatives; the chain rule; differentials; implicit differentiation; parametric differentiation; nth derivative of a function and Leibniz theorem; Rolle's theorem and the mean value theorem; Taylor and Maclaurin series; Indeterminate forms and L'Hopital rule; Maximum and minimum values; curve sketching; Anti-derivative and Standard Integration Integral; Analytic Geometry: straight line, Conic Sections, Solid geometry.

* **MA112: Discrete Mathematics**
  * *Prerequisite:* None
  * *Description:* Foundations of discrete mathematics as they apply to computer science, focusing on providing a solid theoretical foundation for further work. Topics include functions, relations, sets, simple proof techniques, Boolean algebra, propositional logic, digital logic, elementary number theory, fundamentals of counting.

* **MA113: Math-2**
  * *Prerequisite:* Math-1
  * *Description:* Techniques of integration; Definite integrals; the fundamental theorem of calculus; improper Integrals; Area between curves; solids of revolution; arc length; surface areas of revolution; Partial Differentiation; First Order Differential Equations; Second and Higher Order Linear Ordinary Differential Equations; Multiple Integrals; Line and surface integral; Sequences and Infinite Series: Tests of convergence and divergence; Alternating series; Power Series.

* **MA214: Math-3**
  * *Prerequisite:* Math-2
  * *Description:* Matrices: Properties and Algebraic operations; Solution of linear systems of equations; Eigen-value problem: Model matrix and similarity; Cayley-Hamilton theorem and its applications; Harmonic analysis: Real and Complex Fourier Series; Half range and Quarter range expansions; Fourier Integral Transform; Laplace and Inverse Laplace Transforms: Properties and Applications.

### Sixth: Specialization Requirements - Operations Research and Decision Support Department (63 credit hours)
#### 1-3 Applied Sciences (39 compulsory credit hours distributed as follows):

* **DS312: Decision Support and Future Studies Methodologies**
  * *Prerequisites:* Introduction to Operations Research and Decision Support
  * *Description:* Concepts and techniques to construct and implement an effective computer-based decision support systems (DSS) including problem solving, decision-making process; types of DSS (Data/Model/Knowledge DSS); model building and its languages; model selection/integration/execution; types of computer based information systems; system development life cycle; systems analysis and integrated computer-based DSS design methodologies; real life case studies of integrated DSS; foundations of futures studies; future studies methodologies, such as: Scenario approach, environmental scanning, and futures wheel; strategic foresight; software tools and integrated DSS software packages will be stressed used throughout the course.

* **DS313: Computational Intelligence**
  * *Prerequisites:* Linear and Integer Programming & Probability and Statistics-2
  * *Description:* Main components of the field of computational intelligence (CI) such as evolutionary and fuzzy computation; emphasis will be made on the application of CI techniques to real life optimization problems; related heuristic techniques such as Ant Algorithms, Genetic Algorithms, Tabu search, Simulated Annealing are covered; the advantages and limitations as well as the guidelines for selecting the most efficient approach for various types of problems; implementation of CI techniques for various problems will be stressed throughout the course.

* **DS321: Linear and Integer Programming**
  * *Prerequisites:* Introduction to Operations Research and Decision Support & Math-2
  * *Description:* Formulation of problems into linear and integer programs; the graphical solution approach; the simplex method; two-phase method; dual simplex algorithm; revised simplex; duality in linear programming; sensitivity analysis, post-optimal analysis; parametric linear programming; decomposition technique; interior-point method; integer programming algorithms; economic interpretation of solutions; real life applications such as the traveling salesman, transportation and assignment problems; real life case studies and state of art solver software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS322: Non-linear Programming**
  * *Prerequisites:* Linear and Integer Programming
  * *Description:* Definitions and properties of convex functions and sets; optimality conditions of unconstrained and constrained nonlinear programming problems; line search methods without using derivatives; line search methods using derivatives; multidimensional search methods without using derivatives; multidimensional search methods using derivatives; penalty and barrier functions methods; and methods of feasible directions; real life case studies and state of art solver software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS323: Dynamic Programming and Stochastic Modeling**
  * *Prerequisites:* Linear and Integer Programming
  * *Description:* Dynamic programming; stochastic models; queuing theory; multistage decision-making; recursive equations; forward and backward recursion; state variables in dynamic programming; Markov chains and analysis; Markov decision process; random walk; Poisson process; truncated Poisson process; pure birth process; pure death process and birth and death process; queuing systems covering single and multi-stage queuing models; queuing network models; real life case studies and state of art solver software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS331: System Modeling and Simulation**
  * *Prerequisites:* Object-Oriented Programming
  * *Description:* Fundamentals of simulation as a modeling technique; emphasis is on the value of simulation as an experimental tool to support problem solving and decision making; concepts of building a complete simulation study through stochastic discrete event simulation procedures (event, activity, and process based models); Input data analysis; different time advance mechanisms; validation and verification; output data analysis; using output to support decision making are studied through a number of mathematical, industrial and business applications; statistical methods in simulation experiments; implementing different simulation models using computer programming is stressed throughout the course; introduction to software tools for simulation is given; concepts of random number generation and sampling are discussed and used.

* **DS341: Learning From Data**
  * *Prerequisites:* Probability and Statistics-2
  * *Description:* Theoretical and practical aspects related to building data driven models; linear models for regression and classification; kernel machines; neural networks; emphasis will be placed on practical computational complexity and programming aspects of such models; exposition will be made of the problem of over fitting; the bias-variance dilemma; model regularization and model validation.

* **DS352: Production and Operations Management**
  * *Prerequisites:* Decision Support and Future Studies Methodologies
  * *Description:* Introduction to a variety of areas in operations and supply chain management; various operations management decision including service and product design, quality management, process and capacity, facility location, layout design, scheduling, and inventory management; real life case studies and state of art software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS414: Game Theory**
  * *Prerequisites:* Probability and Statistics-2 & Linear and Integer Programming
  * *Description:* Strategy games; rationality; dominance relations; bargaining theory; non-cooperative games; cooperative games; games with incomplete information; repeated games and evolutionary stable strategies; case studies will be used to illustrate the application of game theory to real world problems along with implementing game-theoretic settings.

* **DS415: Decision Theory**
  * *Prerequisites:* Probability and Statistics-2 & Linear and Integer Programming
  * *Description:* Basic concepts of decision making under certainty, risk and uncertainty; decision tables; decision trees; sequential decision-making; opportunity loss; one-time decisions; expected value of information; conditional probability; decision analysis; multiple comparison and multiple ranking methods are explained; approaches to the management of risk and concepts such as uncertainty and variability, Quantifying uncertainty, Probability assessment methods and risk attitudes; real life case studies and state of art software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS424: Multi-objective Programming**
  * *Prerequisites:* Non Linear Programming
  * *Description:* Concepts of multi-objective programming; vector optimization problems; techniques; utility theory; goal programming methods; interactive multi-objective methods and evolutionary algorithms for multi-objective programming; real life case studies and state of art solver software will be used throughout the course.

* **DS425: Network Modeling and Optimization**
  * *Prerequisites:* Dynamic Programming and Stochastic Modeling
  * *Description:* Introduction to network problems in operations research, computer science, electrical engineering and systems engineering; concepts of graph theory, network representations, network transformations, shortest paths algorithms, maximum flows algorithms, minimum cost flows algorithms, generalized network and combinatorial-based network models; real life case studies and algorithms implementation will be stressed throughout the course.
